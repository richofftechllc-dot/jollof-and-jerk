Where It Comes From

Every dish has a
story worth knowing.

Jollof rice has been fought over for generations — Ghana, Nigeria, Senegal all claim it. The real answer is it belongs to the diaspora. Mamadou grew up with it.

Jerk was born in Jamaica — the Maroons, escaped enslaved people who hid in the mountains, preserved the technique of smoking meat with pimento wood and scotch bonnet. It was survival food. Now it's one of the most beloved flavors on earth.

Before opening Jollof & Jerk, Mamadou was Executive Chef at Lesly's Sport Bar & Grill in Falls Church, VA — where his cooking caught the attention of ABC 7 News. Now he's doing it on his own terms, as Owner & CEO.

Every plate at Jollof & Jerk carries that history. We'll tell you the story every time we drop a special.

Jollof Rice

Named after the Jolof Empire of 14th-century Senegal. The dish traveled with the people — to Nigeria, Ghana, Liberia — and each country made it their own. Smoky party jollof is the West African celebration version.

🌍 West Africa — Senegal · Nigeria · Ghana

Jerk Chicken

Created by the Maroons of Jamaica in the Blue Mountains. The word "jerk" comes from the Quechua word "ch'arki" — dried meat. Scotch bonnet and allspice are the non-negotiables. Everything else is style.

🇯🇲 Jamaica — Blue Mountains, 1600s

Oxtail

Once dismissed as scraps — the cuts no one wanted. The African and Caribbean diaspora turned them into the most coveted dish on the menu. Slow-braised for hours. You can't rush it.

🌍 Africa & Caribbean — Diaspora tradition
